<h1>Research</h1> <br>
<img class="w3-image" src="InternshipImage.jpg" alt="Picture of the cover page of the paper I co-published"> <br>
<p>Over the summer, I worked with a professor from the Naval Academy on a research project. The project involved creating software and algorithms <br>
for modeling and predicting the insulin and glucose levels of diabetic patients. My main contribution was optimizing a piece of matlab code to <br>
run faster. The issue was that my mentor had a matlab script that could made a mathematical model of a diabetic patient based on measured values <br>
from the patient, but this program would take somewhere from 2-4 hours to run per patient. This particularly posed a problem to us because we <br>
wanted to generate in the order of several hundred patients in order to analyze how accurate the predictions were across a large data set. We <br>
did not want to wait 50 days in order to create the models; especially if it turned out there was an error in the program. One solution for creating <br>
the dataset would have been to run the processes in parallel, but this would require more processing power, and the application of the research <br>
would be providing a finished tool for doctors to use in prescribing medication for patients, so the solution was not as simple as just using a <br>
larger computer, since we can not influence the computer the end user may use. In order to decrease the time it took to run the program, I focused <br>
on reducing the number of variables the program had to optimise, while still maintaining reasonable accuracy. In the end, I was successful in doing <br>
so, and was able to reduce the run time of the program from hours down to a few minutes. Participating in this research was an amazing experience <br>
for me because it allowed me to apply my knowledge of programming and mathematics to a field I was unfamiliar with. The research also provided <br>
context for concepts I learned in school, such as various techniques in calculus. <br>
</p> <br>

<h1>Rocket</h1> <br>
<img class="w3-image" src="rocket2.jpg" alt="Picture of the cover page of the paper I co-published"> <br>
<img class="w3-image" src="rocketSchem.jpg" alt="Picture of the cover page of the paper I co-published"> <br>
<p>In tenth grade, my science teacher assigned our class to create two small rockets; one powered by compressed air and one powered by an acid-base reaction. <br>
My partner and I decided to combine the two, and create one rocket capable of being propelled by either method. We also decided to go big, with the final rocket <br>
standing almost 4-feet tall (as opposed to the upside-down water bottles many of my classmates created). In order to propel such a large rocket, we decided it <br>
would need to be at a high pressure (our estimates were a couple hundred psi), and the rocket would need to remain light. To do this, we decided to create a <br>
3-foot tall, carbon fiber pressure tank as the centerpiece of the rocket. Carbon fiber met all the criteria were looking for; it was lightweight, high strength, <br>
and could be bought on Amazon. Neither of us knew how to fabricate something out of carbon fiber, so I volunteered to learn. Our first step was to design the <br>
pressure tank and housing, which we did in Autocad Inventor, and ran structural analysis to verify that our pressure tank design could theoretically hold <br>
600psi before undergoing a RUD (rapid unscheduled disassembly). The next steps were to 3d-print the outer casing and pressure tank mold, which we distributed <br>
across several printers at our school and our houses (I forgot to mention, we were on a strict time schedule. Ever hour of production was planned, and we <br>
barely got everything printed in time.) After buying supplies off Amazon, and watching youtube videos about carbon fiber, I set to work making the pressure tank. <br>
Using a hand layup and skinning process, I wrapped a 3d-printed shell in several layers of carbon fiber to create the pressure tank. I remember I put on the last <br>
coat of epoxy with the exact number of hours before the project as was needed to cure the epoxy. After working late into the night and early into the morning <br>
each day on the project, anticipation was high when we went to test it for the teacher. We set up the rocket, pumped it with air, and fsssssss.... Turns out, <br>
carbon fiber is, well, a fiber. And, fibers, like fabrics, are porous. So, as we filled the tank, the air simply leaked out through the carbon fiber skin. For <br>
a couple weeks after the first test, I tried putting on more layers of epoxy to seal the holes, but was unsuccessful. The moral of the story is, although we <br>
were not successful at launching a rocket, we tried things far beyond the requirements, and learned technologies like CAD structural analysis and high-performance <br>
composite fabrication that we would not have learned had we taken the easy road. <br>
</p> <br>

<h1>Robotics</h1> <br>
<img class="w3-image" src="robotics2.jpg" alt="Picture of the cover page of the paper I co-published"> <br>
<p>Both Freshman and Sophomore years of High School, I participated in my school's FTC robotics team; sophomore year as a team captain. <br>
Freshman year, I worked on our robot's autonomous code, and was able to develop a program that would use an IR sensor array to track <br>
an IR emitter. The code would then line up the robot in front of a 3-foot high goal, the robot would autonomously raise its arm, and then <br>
deposit a ball into the goal. Sophomore year, I was assigned a team captain, so I spent the year bouncing around to different stations. Our <br>
robotics organization consists of 3 FTC teams, and one FRC team. The larger FRC team has a dedicated design group that CAD models the robot <br>
before fabrication, and is thus able to work through bugs in the design ahead of time and be more efficient with fabrication. I noticed this, <br>
so I implemented a similar group in my FTC team, and we were the first FTC team at our school to do so; other teams have since followed suit. <br>
We did run into many problems with timing and practicality, but I would say it was overall a success. <br>
